\section{Introduction}
When stars run out of fuel in their cores at the end of their lifespan, the mass and composition of the star dicates what remains. Those under eight solar masses ($<8M_\odot)$ will typically not undergo collapse, becoming a white dwarf. The heaviest stars ($\geq 25M_\odot$) leave behind the famous black hole after going supernova\cite{Heger2003}. In the middle, we get some of the most extreme places in the universe, the neutron star. Neutron stars are an exciting area of study in the field of Astrophysics as they offer insight to exotic environments that aren't reproducible in the lab on earth. These incredibly dense stellar objects were first theorized by Baade and Zwicky. They proposed that the immense release of energy from supernova (a contender for the origin of cosmic rays) is due to the tight packing of neutrons, ''With all reserve we advance the view that a super-nova represents the transition of an ordinary star into a neutron star, consisting mainly of neutrons. Such a star may possess a very small radius and an extremely high density."\cite{Baade} This extremely high density will be explored later as we invoke quantum mechanical effects.
